<p style="text-align:justify;">In this post, I'll explain the mathematical context surrounding my Ph.D. program, which should explain the name of the blog. As a warning, this is directed towards a public of specialists, maybe not of my own area of research, but at least of algebraic geometry or closely related fields. I will return to this post in less or non-technical language later on—as any mathematician knows, this is <em>much</em> harder than speaking to experts.</p>

<p style="text-align:justify;">Let $k$ be a perfect field and let $n$ be a non-negative integer. Let $\mathrm{X}$ be a $k$-scheme and let $\mathrm{E}$ be a vector bundle of rank $n$. We say that $\mathrm{E}$ <em>splits</em> a free line bundle when there exists a vector bundle $\mathrm{E}'$ on $\mathrm{X}$ such that $\mathrm{E}=\mathrm{E}'\times\mathbb{A}^1$. The <em>splitting problem</em> for $\mathrm{X}$ then consists in understanding which vector bundles on $\mathrm{X}$ split a free line bundle, with the idea that these bundles are just as complicated as a direct summand of a lower rank, the latter being usually easier to study. More “philosophically”, this type of problem is of enormous interest considering the information on geometric objects generally carried by vector bundles on them; the splitting problem is closely related to algebraic $\mathrm{K}$-theory in particular.</p>

<p style="text-align:justify;">To study the splitting problem, Barge and Morel introduced a tool in 2000 which they dubbed “groupes de Chow orientés”—this translates to <em>oriented Chow groups</em> in English. The name did not last—in part because, as a cohomological theory for schemes, they do not satisfy the orientation axiom—and they are now called Chow–Witt groups. Here is a taste of their properties. With each line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ on $\mathrm{X}$ is associated an abelian group denoted by $\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}^n(\mathrm{X},\mathcal{L})$ and called the Chow–Witt group of $\mathrm{X}$ associated with $\mathcal{L}$. Any vector bundle $\mathrm{E}$ of rank $n$ and with determinant bundle $\mathcal{L}$ possesses a class $e(\mathrm{E})\in\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}^n(\mathrm{X},\mathcal{L})$ with the property that $e(\mathrm{E})=0$ if $\mathrm{E}$ splits a free line bundle; furthermore, this is an <em>equivalence</em> if $\mathrm{X}$ is connected and $\mathrm{E}$ is of rank $n=\dim(\mathrm{X})$.</p>

<p style="text-align:justify;">Chow–Witt groups are functorial for arbitrary $k$-morphisms between smooth $k$-schemes. More precisely, if $f:\mathrm{Y}\rightarrow\mathrm{X}$ is a $k$-morphism between smooth $k$-schemes, then $f$ induces a morphism $f^{*}:\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}^n(\mathrm{X},\mathcal{L})\rightarrow\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}^n(\mathrm{Y},f^{*}\mathcal{L})$.</p>
