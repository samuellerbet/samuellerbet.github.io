<p style="text-align:justify;">In this post, I'll explain the mathematical context surrounding my Ph.D. program, which should explain the name of the blog. As a warning, this is directed towards a public of, say, master students, maybe not specialised in my own area of research, but at least in algebraic geometry or closely related fields. I will return to this post in less or non-technical language later on—as any mathematician knows, this is <em>much</em> harder than speaking to experts.</p>

<p style="text-align:justify;">I will introduced some terminology first. Let \(\mathrm{X}\) be a scheme and let \(\mathrm{V}\rightarrow\mathrm{X}\) be a vector bundle. We say that \(\mathrm{V}\) <em>splits</em> a free line bundle when there exists a vector bundle \(\mathrm{V}'\) such that \(\mathrm{V}\simeq\mathrm{V}\oplus\mathbb{A}^1\). This reduces the study of \(\mathrm{V}\) to that of \(\mathrm{V}'\) in many situations, thus simplifying it as vector bundles of low rank are easier to investigate. The <em>splitting problem</em> for \(\mathrm{X}\) consists in understanding which vector bundles on \(\mathrm{X}\) split of a free line bundle. Because of the significance of the geometric information on a space carried by vector bundles on that space, this problem is of great interest in general; more specifically, it is closely related to algebraic \(\mathrm{K}\)-theory.</p>

<p style="text-align:justify;">Barge and Morel introduced a major tool in tackling the splitting problem in 2000 which they dubbed “groupes de Chow des cycles orientés” (in English, Chow groups of oriented cycles). The name did not last—in part because as a cohomological theory on schemes, “Chow groups of oriented cycles” do not satisfy the orientation axiom, thus making the terminology awkward—and we know call them <em>Chow–Witt groups</em>. Today, there are a number of ways of defining these groups. The approach followed by Barge and Morel used a Milnor \(\mathrm{K}\)-theory complex whose cohomology computes Chow groups, and a Gersten–Witt complex introduced in the Ph.D. thesis of Schmid. The main difficulty with this approach was that the proof that the Gersten–Witt complex is indeed a complex was missing at the time. Fasel's thesis was devoted to defining these groups using another complex, the Gersten–Witt complex of Balmer–Walter, based on Balmer's derived Witt groups whose functoriality properties implied those of Chow–Witt groups—the latter's name then derived from their link to Chow groups and Witt groups respectively.</p>

<p style="text-align:justify;">Let then \(k\) be a perfect field; we denote by \(\mathsf{Sm}/k\) the category whose objects are smooth \(k\)-schemes and whose morphisms are <em>arbitrary</em> morphisms of k-schemes. Let \(n\) be an integer, let \(\mathrm{X}\) be a smooth \(k\)-scheme and let \(\mathcal{L}\) be a line bundle on \(\mathrm{X}\). With these data is associated a group \(\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}^n(\mathrm{X},\mathcal{L})\)—which depends solely on the isomorphism class of \(\mathcal{L}\)—in which lives, for any vector bundle \(\mathrm{V}\) on \(\mathrm{X}\) of rank \(n\) and with determinant bundle \(\mathcal{L}\), a class \(e(\mathrm{V})\) called the <em>Euler class</em> of \(\mathrm{V}\); when \(\mathcal{L}\) is the trivial line bundle, we omit it from the notation and simply write \(\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}^n(\mathrm{X})\). Chow–Witt groups possess properties very similar to those of Chow groups. For instance, they are contravariantly functorial for arbitrary morphisms of \(k\)-schemes: if \(f:\mathrm{Y}\rightarrow\mathrm{X}\) is a \(k\)-morphism between smooth \(k\)-schemes, then \(f\) induces a map \(f^*:\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}^n(\mathrm{X},\mathcal{L})\rightarrow\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}^n(\mathrm{Y},f^*\mathcal{L})\) which is functorial in \(f\). The Euler class is also functorial, i.e. for any vector bundle \(\mathrm{V}\) on \(\mathrm{X}\), we have \(e(f^*\mathrm{V})=f^*e(\mathrm{V})\). Furthermore, similarly to Chow groups, Chow–Witt groups satisfy localisation exact sequences. Moreover, they are partially covariantly functorial, though in a less pleasant way than Chow groups. More precisely, let \(p:\mathrm{Y}\rightarrow\mathrm{X}\) be a proper morphism between smooth \(k\)-schemes which we assume to be of constant relative dimension \(d\) and let \(\mathcal{M}\) be a line bundle on \(\mathrm{Y}\). Then there exists a morphism \(p_*:\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}^{n+d}(\mathrm{Y},\mathcal{M})\rightarrow\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}^{n}(\mathrm{X},\mathcal{L})\) (with the usual functoriality properties) only if $$\mathcal{M}\otimes p^*\mathcal{L}^{-1}=\omega_{\mathrm{Y}/k}\otimes p^*\omega_{\mathrm{X}/k}^{-1}$$ in \(\mathrm{Pic}(\mathrm{Y})/2\), the quotient of the Picard group of \(\mathrm{Y}\) by the multiplication by \(2\), where \(\omega_{\mathrm{Z}/k}\) is the determinant bundle of the sheaf of differentials of the smooth \(k\)-scheme \(\mathrm{Z}\). Finally, there is a graded commutative product structure on the Chow–Witt groups of \(\mathrm{X}\), the <em>intersection product</em> $$\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}^n(\mathrm{X},\mathcal{L})\otimes\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}^m(\mathrm{X},\mathcal{M})\rightarrow\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}^{n+m}(\mathrm{X},\mathcal{L}\otimes\mathcal{N}).$$ Thus if we let \[\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}(\mathrm{X})=\bigoplus_{n\in\mathbb{N}}\bigoplus_{\mathcal{L}\in\mathrm{Pic}(\mathrm{X})}\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}^n(\mathrm{X},\mathcal{L}),\] then we obtain a contravariant functor \(\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}(\text{–})\) from the category \(\mathsf{Sm}/k\) to the category of graded commutative rings. We call \(\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}(\mathrm{X})\) the total Chow–Witt ring of \(\mathrm{X}\).</p>

<p style="text-align:justify;">The interest of the Euler class in studying the splitting problem is the following property. If \(\mathrm{V}\) is a vector bundle on a smooth \(k\)-scheme \(\mathrm{X}\) and if \(\mathrm{V}\) splits, then \(e(\mathrm{V})\) vanishes; furthermore, the converse is true if in addition \(\mathrm{X}\) is connected and \(\mathrm{V}\) is of rank \(\dim\mathrm{X}\)—or, more generally, if, seen as locally constant functions on \(\mathrm{X}\), the rank of \(\mathrm{V}\) and the dimension of \(\mathrm{X}\) agree (in fact, the Chow–Witt groups of \(\mathrm{X}\) split as the direct sum of the Chow–Witt groups of the connected components if \(\mathrm{X}\) is not connected, each of which is irreducible as \(\mathrm{X}\) is smooth, and the Euler class of \(\mathrm{V}\) is the direct sum of the Euler classes of the restrictions of \(\mathrm{V}\) to each connected component). The bound \(\dim(\mathrm{X})\) is particularly relevant in light of Serre's theorem which asserts that a vector bundle of rank \(n>d\) on a smooth affine \(k\)-scheme splits a line bundle.</p>

<p style="text-align:justify;">Chow–Witt groups possess an additional property which is in fact the reason for the blog's title (finally!). Let \(\mathrm{X}\) be a smooth \(k\)-scheme. We denote by \(\mathrm{CH}^n(\mathrm{X})\) the \(n\)-th Chow group of \(\mathrm{X}\): its elements are classes of cycles of codimension \(n\) (formal sums of irreducible subvarieties of codimension \(n\)) under rational equivalence, which means that divisors of rational functions exactly generate the kernel of the quotient map from the free abelian group on cycles of codimension \(n\) to \(\mathrm{CH}^n(\mathrm{X})\). by \(\mathrm{CH}(\mathrm{X})\) the total Chow ring of \(\mathrm{X}\), that is, the direct sum of the groups \(\mathrm{CH}^n(\mathrm{X})\) endowed with the usual intersection product of cycles. Then \(\mathrm{CH}(\mathrm{X})\) is a graded commutative ring and the usual pullback of cycles induces a functor \(\mathrm{CH}(\text{–})\) from the category \(\mathsf{Sm}/k\) to the category of graded commutative rings, and there exists a (necessarily non-trivial, since the objets in question are rings) natural transformation from \(\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}(\text{–})\) to \(\mathrm{CH}(\text{–})\). Thus Chow–Witt groups are in a sense a refinement of Chow groups. Furthermore, the Euler class of a vector bundle \(\mathrm{V}\) of rank \(n\) on \(\mathrm{X}\) is sent to the top Chern class \(c_n(\mathrm{V})\in\mathrm{CH}^n(\mathrm{X})\) of \(\mathrm{V}\), so that the former also refines the latter.</p>

<p style="text-align:justify;">How may one think about this refinement of Chow groups into Chow–Witt groups? To simplify the discussion, we will concentrate on untwisted Chow–Witt groups, that is, on the groups \(\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}^n(\mathrm{X})\). An element of \(\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}^n(\mathrm{X})\) is roughly speaking a cycle of codimension \(n\), endowed with a (finite-dimensional, non-degenerate) symmetric bilinear form on the function field of each irreducible subvariety composing the cycle, with an additional ramification condition on these symmetric bilnear forms—they must in fact be unramified. The map \(\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}^n(\mathrm{X})\rightarrow\mathrm{CH}^n(\mathrm{X})\) then forgets the underlying symmetric bilinear forms and associates the cycle with the given element of the left-hand side: we call it the <em>suport</em> of the given element of \(\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}^n(\mathrm{X})\).</p>

<p style="text-align:justify;">Recall then that if \(\mathrm{K}\) is a field of characteristic different from \(2\), the association \(b\mapsto(x\mapsto b(x,x))\) induces a bijection between the set of finite-dimensional, non-degenerate symmetric bilinear forms over \(\mathrm{K}\) and the set of finite-dimensional, non-degenerate quadratic forms over \(\mathrm{K}\). Therefore, when \(k\) is of characteristic different from \(2\), an elemnt of \(\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}^n(\mathrm{X})\) is roughly speaking an cycle with quadratic forms on each irreducible subvariety of the cycle; <em>thus the name quadratic cycles</em>!</p>

<p style="text-align:justify;">Because of the additional datum of symmetric bilinear forms on the function fields of the cycle, one easily conceives that the support map from Chow–Witt groups to Chow groups may not be injective and this is indeed the case in general. Interestingly, because of the ramification condition, not every cycle of codimension \(n\) on \(\mathrm{X}\) is the support of an element of \(\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}^n(\mathrm{X})\), so that the map \(\widetilde{\mathrm{CH}}^n(\mathrm{X})\rightarrow\mathrm{CH}^n(\mathrm{X})\) is not surjective either (again, in general).</p>

<p style="text-align:justify;">Now, what about my Ph.D. project? Classically, there is a <em>cycle class map</em> from Chow groups to singular cohomology. Let me be more precise. If \(\mathrm{X}\) is a complex algebraic variety, we denote by \(\mathrm{X}(\mathbb{C})\) the set of complex point or complex locus of \(\mathrm{X}\). It has a natural complex analytic space structure, which is evident if \(\mathrm{X}\) is an affine variety, the general case being obtained by gluing. In particular, it is reasonable to take the singular cohomology of the complex locus of \(\mathrm{X}\). Furthermore, \(\mathrm{X}(\mathbb{C})\) is a complex analytic <em>variety</em> if in addition \(\mathrm{X}\) is smooth as an complex algebraic variety. Let then \(\mathrm{X}\) be a smooth complex (irreducible) algebraic variety of dimension \(d\). Then, associate with any (irreducible) subvariety \(\mathrm{Y}\) of codimension \(n\) of \(\mathrm{X}\) the fundamental class \([\mathrm{Y}(\mathbb{C})]\) of the set of complex points of \(\mathrm{Y}\) viewed as a (closed) subvariety of the analytic complex variety \(\mathrm{X}(\mathbb{C})\): this is an element of the homology group \(\mathrm{H}_{2d-2n}(\mathrm{X}(\mathbb{C}),\mathbb{Z})\). By Poincaré duality—recall that \(\mathrm{X}\) is smooth and \(\mathrm{X}(\mathbb{C})\) complex analytic, thus canonically oriented—, one obtains a class \(c(\mathrm{Y})\) in the cohomology group \(\mathrm{H}^{2n}(\mathrm{X}(\mathbb{C}),\mathbb{Z})\). This assignment extends trivially to the free abelian group on cycles of codimension \(n\) by the very definition of the free abelian group; one can in fact show that it descends to  a well-defined group homomorphism \(c:\mathrm{CH}^n(\mathrm{X})\rightarrow\mathrm{H}^{2n}(\mathrm{X}(\mathbb{C}),\mathbb{Z})\) which is called the cycle class map (for obvious reasons). The cycle class maps transforms intersection products into cup products and thus induces a morphism \(c:\mathrm{CH}(\mathrm{X})\rightarrow\mathrm{H}(\mathrm{X}(\mathbb{C}),\mathbb{Z})\) of graded commutative rings.</p>

<p style="text-align:justify;">Now, what about my Ph.D. project? Classically, there is a <em>cycle class map</em> from Chow groups to singular cohomology. More precisely, let \(\mathrm{X}\) be a smooth complex (irreducible) algebraic variety of dimension \(d\). Then, associate with any (irreducible) subvariety \(\mathrm{Y}\) of codimension \(n\) of \(\mathrm{X}\) the fundamental class \([\mathrm{Y}(\mathbb{C})]\) of the set of complex points of \(\mathrm{Y}\) viewed as a (closed) subvariety of the analytic complex variety \(\mathrm{X}(\mathbb{C})\): this is an element of the homology group \(\mathrm{H}_{2d-2n}(\mathrm{X}(\mathbb{C}),\mathbb{Z})\). By Poincaré duality—recall that \(\mathrm{X}\) is smooth and complex analytic, thus canonically oriented—, one obtains a class \(c(\mathrm{Y})\) in the cohomology group \(\mathrm{H}^{2n}(\mathrm{X}(\mathbb{C}),\mathbb{Z})\). This assignment extends trivially to the free abelian group on cycles of codimension \(n\) by the very definition of the free abelian group; one can in fact show that it descends to  a well-defined group homomorphism \(c:\mathrm{CH}^n(\mathrm{X})\rightarrow\mathrm{H}^{2n}(\mathrm{X}(\mathbb{C}),\mathbb{Z})\) which is called the cycle class map (for obvious reasons).</p>

<p style="text-align:justify;">Now, say you are interested in <em>real</em> algebraic varieties. These are more difficult to deal with than complex algebraic varieties, for many reasons, not the least of which is that algebraically closed fields are easier to study than other kinds of fields. For example, some real algebraic varieties have an empty real locus—consider the conic \(\{x^2+y^2=-1\}\subseteq\mathbb{A}^2=\mathrm{Spec}\;\mathbb{R}[x,y]\). Yet there is growing interest in real algebraic geometry, both for concrete applications in physics or computer science and in pure maths, for example because, since the Galois group of the extension \(\mathbb{C}/\mathbb{R}\) is as simple as possible without being trivial, and since it acts on most objects studied by algebraic geometers for functoriality reasons, real algebraic varieties are the natural step from complex algebraic varieties (if, say, one is not interested in arithmetic problems.)</p>
